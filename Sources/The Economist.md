# The Economist

## Overview
The Economist is a weekly international news magazine known for its distinctive data journalism. Their visual data team produces the "Graphic Detail" section and daily chart, setting the standard for editorial chart design that communicates a single clear insight. Their restrained, information-dense style has influenced a generation of data journalists and chart designers.

## Signature Visual Style
- **Color palette**: Iconic red (#E3120B) for branding, with charts using highly restrained 1-2 color schemes. Primary blue for data, red for emphasis only. Grey for secondary data and context
- **Typography**: Economist serif (Econ Sans / Milo) for headlines, clean sans-serif for chart labels and axes. Strong typographic hierarchy between title, subtitle, and annotation
- **Layout**: Single chart per story, optimized for print column width. Title carries the insight, subtitle provides context, chart proves it. Generous annotation space
- **Tone**: Opinionated, witty, intellectually rigorous. Charts make arguments, not just display data

## Strengths
- One insight per chart — ruthless editorial discipline in deciding what a chart should say
- Restrained color use (typically 1-2 colors) that forces clarity of encoding
- Title as takeaway: headline states the conclusion, chart provides the evidence
- Consistent style guide that makes any Economist chart instantly recognizable
- Exceptional annotation practice — every chart tells you what to look at and why

## Chart Types They Favor
- [[Line Chart]] — trend stories, often with a single highlighted series against grey context lines
- [[Bar Chart]] — rankings and comparisons, both horizontal and vertical, always directly labeled
- [[Scatter Plot]] — relationship stories (GDP vs X, population vs Y) with selective labeling of outliers
- [[Choropleth Map]] — geographic stories with carefully chosen sequential color scales
- [[Small Multiples]] — comparing patterns across countries or categories in compact grid layouts

## Design Patterns Worth Studying
- **Title-as-insight**: The chart title states the takeaway ("Solar is now the cheapest electricity in history") rather than describing the data ("Solar electricity costs over time")
- **Annotated single-insight charts**: Heavy use of direct labels, callout annotations, and reference lines to guide the reader to the key finding
- **Restrained color discipline**: Maximum 2-3 colors per chart, using grey to suppress secondary information and color to highlight the story
- **Print-first design**: Charts designed to work at newspaper column width, forcing economy of design that translates beautifully to digital

## Energy & Climate Relevance
The Economist covers energy and climate extensively with data-driven reporting. Their charts on carbon pricing, renewable costs, fossil fuel stranding, and emissions trajectories reach a highly influential readership of policymakers and business leaders. Excellent reference for how to communicate climate data to decision-makers concisely.

## Key Reports / Products
- **Graphic Detail** — Weekly full-page data visualization on a current topic
- **Daily Chart** — One chart per day published on the website and app with brief analysis
- **Climate Issue** — Annual special edition focused on climate and energy data
- **The World Ahead** — Annual forecast publication with data-heavy projections
- **Economist Intelligence Unit (EIU)** — Separate research arm with country-level data and forecasts

## Data Access
- Charts viewable with Economist subscription (paywall)
- No raw data download; they visualize third-party data with attribution
- EIU provides paid data subscriptions for country-level indicators
- Style guide principles documented in talks and blog posts by their data team
- GitHub repositories occasionally shared for methodology transparency
