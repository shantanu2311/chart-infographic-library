# Global Carbon Project

## Overview
The Global Carbon Project (GCP) is an international research consortium that quantifies global carbon sources and sinks, publishing the annual Global Carbon Budget. Established in 2001 under the World Climate Research Programme, GCP provides the definitive accounting of where carbon comes from, where it goes, and how much budget remains for climate targets. Their data underpins IPCC assessments and virtually every global emissions analysis.

## Signature Visual Style
- **Color palette**: Academic but accessible. Source-specific encoding: fossil fuels=#404040 (dark grey), land use change=#8B4513 (brown), ocean sink=#1E90FF (blue), land sink=#228B22 (green). Red for emissions, blue/green for sinks. Clean white backgrounds
- **Typography**: Academic sans-serif, clean axis labels, extensive figure captions typical of scientific publications. Moderate density with clear legends
- **Layout**: Publication-grade figures designed for journal articles and IPCC reports. Stand-alone charts with comprehensive captions. Infographic summaries for media
- **Tone**: Scientific, precise, measured. Data speaks without editorializing, but the implications are clear

## Strengths
- The single most authoritative source for global CO2 emissions data, cited by every major institution
- Extraordinary historical depth — fossil fuel emissions back to 1750, atmospheric CO2 to 800,000 years
- Carbon budget framework that translates emissions into remaining temperature headroom
- Rigorous uncertainty quantification on all estimates with clear methodology
- Annual release cycle creates a reliable media moment that drives global awareness

## Chart Types They Favor
- [[Line Chart]] — CO2 atmospheric concentration over time (the Keeling Curve and its extensions)
- [[Stacked Area Chart]] — global emissions decomposed by source (coal, oil, gas, cement, land use) and by region
- [[Waterfall Chart]] — carbon budget remaining: starting budget minus cumulative emissions = remaining allowance
- [[Choropleth Map]] — country-level emissions, per-capita emissions, and emissions growth rates

## Design Patterns Worth Studying
- **Annual Global Carbon Budget release**: A coordinated multi-chart publication that tells the complete carbon story — sources, sinks, atmospheric growth, and budget implications — in a standardized format updated each year
- **Historical emissions reconstruction**: Stitching together data from multiple eras (pre-industrial, industrial, modern) into continuous time series with transparent uncertainty bands
- **Source decomposition**: Breaking total emissions into fuel types and regions simultaneously using nested stacked areas or small multiples
- **Carbon budget countdown**: Visualizing the remaining carbon budget as a diminishing bar or countdown, creating urgency through simple arithmetic

## Energy & Climate Relevance
GCP is the foundational data source for all GHG inventory work. Their emission factors, historical baselines, and carbon budget framework directly inform Scope 1 calculations, science-based target setting, and climate scenario analysis. Any GHG tool that contextualizes facility-level emissions against global trends relies on GCP data.

## Key Reports / Products
- **Global Carbon Budget** — Annual comprehensive assessment of CO2 sources, sinks, and atmospheric growth (published in Earth System Science Data)
- **Global Methane Budget** — Periodic assessment of CH4 sources and sinks
- **Global Nitrous Oxide Budget** — Periodic assessment of N2O sources and sinks
- **Carbon Atlas** — Interactive website for exploring emissions by country and sector
- **Supplemental data tables** — Excel files with full country-level time series accompanying each budget release

## Data Access
- All data freely available under open access from ICOS Carbon Portal and GCP website
- Excel/CSV downloads for country-level fossil fuel emissions (CDIAC/GCP dataset)
- Data published in peer-reviewed journals with full methodology
- Global Carbon Atlas provides interactive exploration of all datasets
- No formal API, but structured data files enable programmatic access
