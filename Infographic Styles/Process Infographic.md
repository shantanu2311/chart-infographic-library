# Process Infographic

## What It Is
A step-by-step infographic format that breaks down a complex process, workflow, or mechanism into sequential stages. It uses directional flow — arrows, numbered steps, or connected nodes — to guide the reader through how something works from start to finish. This format excels at demystifying opaque institutional processes and technical workflows for non-specialist audiences.

## Key Characteristics
- Clear directional flow with numbered or labeled stages
- Arrows, connectors, or flow lines linking each step to the next
- Icon or illustration per step to provide visual anchoring
- Decision points or branching paths where processes diverge
- Input/output labels showing what enters and exits each stage

## Best Use Cases
- Explaining the carbon credit lifecycle from project development to retirement
- Mapping the GHG inventory process (data collection, calculation, verification, reporting)
- Illustrating MDB project approval pipelines from concept note to disbursement
- Showing CCUS process chains from capture to transport to storage
- Detailing green bond issuance steps from framework to second-party opinion to listing
- Visualizing Article 6 corresponding adjustments and ITMO transfer mechanism

### Energy & Climate Applications
- Carbon credit lifecycle: project design, validation, monitoring, verification, issuance, trading, retirement
- CBAM compliance flow: importer registration, embedded emissions calculation, certificate purchase, surrender
- Green hydrogen production chain: electrolyzer input, purification, compression, transport, end use
- ETS cap-and-trade mechanism: allowance allocation, trading, compliance surrender, penalty
- Climate finance disbursement: pledged, committed, approved, disbursed, verified impact

## Structure & Layout
The most common layout is a horizontal left-to-right flow for 4-7 steps, or a vertical top-to-bottom flow for longer processes (8+ steps). Each step occupies a card or node containing an icon, step number, title, and 1-2 sentence description. Arrows between nodes are the critical structural element — they should be visually prominent and consistent. For processes with decision points, diamond-shaped nodes with yes/no branches are standard. A header introduces the process purpose, and a footer summarizes the outcome or output. Side annotations can highlight duration, responsible parties, or common failure points at specific stages.

## Design Tips
> [!tip] Best Practices
> - Limit to 5-8 main steps; nest sub-steps within expandable sections rather than cluttering the main flow
> - Use consistent node sizing — resist the temptation to make "important" steps larger, as it breaks flow readability
> - Color-code by actor or phase (e.g., project developer = teal, verifier = orange, registry = purple)
> - Add estimated time durations above each arrow to set expectations
> - Include a "common pitfalls" callout box at the step where processes most frequently stall

## When NOT to Use
> [!warning] Avoid When
> - The process is non-linear with heavy feedback loops (use a network or system diagram instead)
> - The goal is comparing two processes side-by-side (use a comparison infographic)
> - The audience already understands the process and needs data, not explanation

## Recommended Chart Types
- [[Sankey Diagram]] — showing material or energy flows through a process with volume proportionality
- [[Funnel Chart]] — illustrating progressive narrowing (e.g., projects submitted vs approved vs funded vs completed)
