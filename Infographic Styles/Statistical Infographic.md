# Statistical Infographic

## What It Is
A data-heavy infographic format that leads with numbers, percentages, and key figures to tell a quantitative story. The visual hierarchy is built around bold statistics supported by charts, icons, and minimal narrative text. It is the workhorse format for communicating research findings, annual reports, and sector-level summaries where credibility hinges on hard data.

## Key Characteristics
- Large, bold hero numbers as focal anchors (e.g., "42 GtCO2e" or "$1.8 trillion")
- High chart-to-text ratio with minimal prose
- Color-coded sections grouping related metrics
- Icon-stat pairings for quick scanning
- Clear source citations and methodology notes at the footer

## Best Use Cases
- Summarising annual emissions inventories or energy balances
- Highlighting key findings from IEA World Energy Outlook or IRENA reports
- Presenting carbon market volumes, prices, and transaction counts
- MDB annual report infographic summaries (e.g., World Bank climate finance flows)
- ESG disclosure snapshots for corporate sustainability reports

### Energy & Climate Applications
- Global GHG emissions breakdown by sector and gas (CO2, CH4, N2O)
- IEA electricity generation mix: fossil vs renewable share year-over-year
- Carbon credit issuance and retirement volumes across voluntary and compliance markets
- MDB climate finance commitments vs disbursements
- Country-level renewable energy capacity additions in GW

## Structure & Layout
Typically uses a vertical scroll layout divided into 3-5 thematic sections. Each section opens with a hero statistic in large typography, followed by 1-2 supporting charts and 2-3 secondary stats with icon pairings. A header banner sets the topic and timeframe. The footer contains data sources, methodology notes, and organizational branding. Whitespace is used generously to prevent cognitive overload despite high data density.

## Design Tips
> [!tip] Best Practices
> - Lead with the single most impactful number — place it above the fold
> - Use no more than 3-4 colors to maintain visual coherence across charts
> - Round numbers for readability (say "~40 GtCO2e" not "39.73 GtCO2e") unless precision matters
> - Always include the base year, units, and data source next to every statistic
> - Use consistent icon style (outlined or filled, not mixed) for stat-icon pairings

## When NOT to Use
> [!warning] Avoid When
> - The story is primarily qualitative or narrative (use a timeline or process infographic instead)
> - You have fewer than 5 distinct data points — a simple chart slide would suffice
> - The audience needs to understand causation or process flow, not just magnitudes

## Recommended Chart Types
- [[Bar Chart]] — comparing emissions across countries, sectors, or fuel types
- [[Donut Chart]] — showing composition shares (e.g., energy mix, Scope 1/2/3 split)
- [[Waffle Chart]] — making percentages tangible with grid-based part-to-whole display
- [[Line Chart]] — embedding small trend sparklines alongside hero numbers
