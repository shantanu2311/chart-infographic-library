# Comparison Infographic

## What It Is
A side-by-side analysis format that places two or more options, systems, or entities in parallel to highlight differences, trade-offs, and relative strengths. It uses visual symmetry and consistent metrics to enable rapid evaluation. This format is indispensable in policy analysis and investment decision-making where stakeholders must weigh competing alternatives on multiple dimensions simultaneously.

## Key Characteristics
- Symmetric layout with parallel columns or mirrored sections
- Consistent comparison criteria applied uniformly across all items
- Visual indicators (checkmarks, scales, color gradients) for quick scoring
- Shared axis or baseline enabling direct metric-to-metric comparison
- Summary verdict or key takeaway at the bottom

## Best Use Cases
- Fossil fuel vs renewable energy levelized cost of electricity (LCOE) analysis
- ETS (cap-and-trade) vs carbon tax policy mechanism comparison
- Solar vs wind vs nuclear on cost, capacity factor, land use, and lifecycle emissions
- Country NDC ambition comparisons across G20 nations
- Voluntary carbon market vs compliance carbon market structure and pricing

### Energy & Climate Applications
- LCOE comparison across solar PV, onshore wind, offshore wind, nuclear, gas CCGT, and coal
- Green hydrogen vs blue hydrogen vs grey hydrogen on cost, emissions intensity, and scalability
- Battery EVs vs fuel cell EVs vs ICE vehicles on total cost of ownership and lifecycle emissions
- Scope 1 vs Scope 2 vs Scope 3 reporting requirements and data availability
- CBAM vs domestic carbon pricing: coverage, border adjustment mechanics, WTO compatibility

## Structure & Layout
The classic layout uses two or three vertical columns, one per option, with a shared set of horizontal comparison rows. Each row represents a criterion (cost, emissions, scalability, maturity, etc.) with visual indicators — bar segments, icon ratings, or color-coded cells. A header identifies each option with its logo or representative icon. For more than three options, a matrix layout with a horizontal scroll or paginated cards works better than cramming columns. The bottom section synthesizes the comparison into a verdict, recommendation, or "best for" classification per use case.

## Design Tips
> [!tip] Best Practices
> - Use identical visual weight and space for each option — asymmetric sizing implies bias
> - Limit comparison criteria to 5-8 rows; beyond that, group into categories with sub-rows
> - Apply a consistent color per option throughout the entire infographic, not per criterion
> - Include a "Best For" summary row at the bottom mapping each option to its ideal use case
> - Normalize units before comparing — do not put MW next to GW or $/kWh next to INR/unit without conversion

## When NOT to Use
> [!warning] Avoid When
> - Comparing more than 5 items on more than 8 criteria (use an interactive dashboard or table instead)
> - One option is clearly superior on all dimensions — a comparison format implies meaningful trade-offs
> - The audience needs to understand how something works before evaluating alternatives (use a process infographic first)

## Recommended Chart Types
- [[Bar Chart]] — grouped or stacked bars for direct metric comparison across options
- [[Dumbbell Chart]] — showing the gap between two values (e.g., cost range, emissions delta)
- [[Radar Chart]] — multi-dimensional profiling of each option across 5-8 criteria on a single shape
- [[Bullet Chart]] — showing actual vs target or benchmark performance per metric per option
