# Timeline Infographic

## What It Is
A chronological infographic format that arranges events, milestones, or phases along a time axis to show progression, evolution, or historical context. It transforms complex policy histories and technology adoption narratives into scannable visual sequences. The format leverages our innate understanding of linear time to make multi-decade stories immediately graspable.

## Key Characteristics
- Central timeline axis (horizontal or vertical) as the structural spine
- Milestone markers with dates, icons, and brief descriptions
- Visual differentiation between eras or phases using color bands or section breaks
- Progressive scaling — more detail for recent periods, compressed for earlier history
- Connector lines or arrows showing cause-and-effect relationships between events

## Best Use Cases
- Mapping the evolution of UNFCCC negotiations from Rio 1992 through COP30
- Charting the energy transition from coal dominance to renewable parity
- Showing the Paris Agreement to Glasgow to Dubai to Belem progression of climate ambition
- Documenting carbon market evolution from Kyoto mechanisms to Article 6
- Illustrating technology adoption curves for solar PV, wind, EVs, or battery storage

### Energy & Climate Applications
- COP milestones: key decisions, pledges, and mechanisms adopted at each summit
- Solar PV cost decline trajectory from $76/W (1977) to $0.20/W (2024)
- History of carbon pricing: EU ETS phases, regional schemes, CBAM introduction
- MDB climate finance evolution: creation of Green Climate Fund, adaptation windows, loss & damage fund
- Hydrogen strategy announcements by country and year

## Structure & Layout
Vertical timelines work best for mobile and long histories (20+ events). Horizontal timelines suit print and shorter sequences (5-12 events). The axis is typically centered with alternating left-right event cards to maximize space. Each card contains a date, headline, 1-2 sentence description, and an optional icon or thumbnail. Phase dividers (colored bands or labeled brackets) group events into eras. A legend at the top explains color coding. Start and end points are visually emphasized — the start with context-setting and the end with a forward-looking call to action or projection.

## Design Tips
> [!tip] Best Practices
> - Maintain consistent spacing proportional to time intervals — do not compress 20 years into the same space as 2 years without visual indication
> - Use a maximum of 12-15 milestones; beyond that, group minor events into phase summaries
> - Color-code by theme (policy = blue, technology = green, finance = gold) rather than by time period
> - Add a "You Are Here" marker for present-day context when showing projections
> - Include a mini data chart (sparkline or small bar) alongside key milestones to show quantitative impact

## When NOT to Use
> [!warning] Avoid When
> - Events lack meaningful chronological relationships (use a list or comparison instead)
> - The data is primarily quantitative with no narrative arc (use a statistical infographic)
> - You need to show simultaneous parallel tracks with heavy interdependencies (use a process or network diagram)
