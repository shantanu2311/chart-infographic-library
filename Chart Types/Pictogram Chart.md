# Pictogram Chart

## What It Is
A pictogram chart uses repeated icons or symbols to represent quantities, where each icon stands for a fixed unit count. Instead of abstract bars or slices, recognizable images (people, trees, lightbulbs, cars) make the data immediately relatable. This approach leverages the ISOTYPE tradition pioneered by Otto Neurath, making statistics accessible to audiences regardless of numeracy level.

## Best Data Types
- Counts and whole numbers that divide evenly into icon units
- Proportions and percentages (e.g., 7 out of 10 icons filled)
- Simple comparisons between 2-5 categories
- Survey results and population statistics
- Before/after comparisons using filled vs. unfilled icons

## When to Use
- Communicating statistics to non-technical or public audiences
- Infographics and reports where visual engagement matters
- Showing proportions in a more intuitive way than [[Pie Chart]] or [[Bar Chart]]
- Making large numbers tangible (1 icon = 1 million tonnes CO2)
- Social media and presentation contexts where visual impact drives the message

## When NOT to Use
> [!warning] Avoid When
> - Precision matters (each icon represents a rounded unit)
> - You have many categories or complex breakdowns
> - Values do not divide cleanly into icon units (partial icons are confusing)
> - The audience expects analytical charts, not infographic-style visuals
> - Data changes frequently and the chart must update dynamically

## Real-World Use Cases
- Public health campaigns (1 in 4 people affected, shown as 4 person icons with 1 highlighted)
- Election results showing seat counts as filled/unfilled icons
- Survey findings (8 out of 10 customers recommend)
- Population statistics in educational materials

### Energy & Climate Applications
- 1 icon = 1 GW of installed solar capacity, comparing countries
- People affected by energy poverty (icon rows showing millions impacted)
- EV adoption: out of 100 vehicle icons, how many are electric
- Trees required to offset 1 tonne of CO2 (visual scale of carbon sequestration)
- Household energy sources: icons showing fuel mix per 100 homes in rural India

## Visual Style Variations
- **Grid array** — icons arranged in a uniform grid, filled/unfilled to show proportion
- **Stacked rows** — icons in rows with color coding per category
- **Scaled icons** — single large icon sized proportionally (less precise but visually bold)
- **Partial fill** — last icon partially filled to represent fractional values

## Related Charts
- [[Waffle Chart]] — grid of squares instead of icons; similar concept, more abstract
- [[Pie Chart]] — shows proportions but less intuitive for general audiences
- [[Bar Chart]] — more precise but less visually engaging for public communication
