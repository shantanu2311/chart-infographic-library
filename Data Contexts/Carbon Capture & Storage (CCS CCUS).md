# Carbon Capture & Storage (CCS / CCUS)

## Overview

Carbon capture, utilisation, and storage (CCUS) encompasses technologies that capture CO2 from point sources (power plants, cement, steel, chemicals) or directly from the atmosphere (direct air capture / DAC), then transport it for permanent geological storage or utilisation in products. CCUS is considered essential for net-zero pathways, particularly for hard-to-abate industrial emissions. Global operational capture capacity reached approximately 50 MtCO2/yr in 2024, with a large pipeline of announced projects targeting 300+ MtCO2/yr by 2030.

## Key Data Types

- Project-level data: facility name, capture source, technology type, capacity (MtCO2/yr), status
- Capture cost data by source type and technology ($/tCO2)
- Storage site characterisation: geology type, capacity, injectivity, monitoring data
- CO2 transport infrastructure: pipelines, shipping routes, terminals
- DAC facility data: technology vendor, energy source, capacity
- Utilisation pathway data: EOR, mineralisation, synthetic fuels, building materials
- Policy and incentive data: 45Q credits, EU Innovation Fund, contracts for difference

## Key Metrics & Indicators

- Operational capture capacity (MtCO2/yr)
- Project pipeline by development status (operating, under construction, FID, advanced development, early development)
- Capture cost by source type ($/tCO2: power, cement, steel, DAC)
- DAC installed capacity (tCO2/yr) and pipeline
- CO2 storage resource estimate (GtCO2) by basin
- Annual CO2 stored (MtCO2/yr)
- CCUS project announced investment ($bn)
- Policy incentive value ($/tCO2: 45Q, CfD, EU ETS + Innovation Fund)

- CO2 Storage Resource Catalogue (CO2SRC) — Global geological storage estimates
- IEA Greenhouse Gas R&D Programme (IEAGHG) — Technical reports and cost studies
- Carbon Engineering / Climeworks / 1PointFive — DAC project disclosures
