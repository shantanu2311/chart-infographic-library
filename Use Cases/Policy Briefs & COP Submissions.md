# Policy Briefs & COP Submissions

## Overview
Policy briefs and COP (Conference of the Parties) submissions are formal documents prepared for policymakers, negotiators, and government officials involved in climate governance. These visualizations must distill complex scientific and economic data into precise, authoritative figures that can withstand scrutiny and inform high-stakes decisions. Every chart in this context carries the weight of potential policy action affecting millions.

## Audience
- National and sub-national policymakers
- UNFCCC negotiators and delegation members
- Government ministry officials (environment, energy, finance)
- Multilateral institution staff (World Bank, UNDP, GEF)
- Parliamentary committee members and legislative analysts

## Key Requirements
- Precision and accuracy with full source attribution and uncertainty ranges
- Restrained, professional color palettes suitable for black-and-white printing
- Print-friendly layouts at A4/Letter size with legible fonts at 9pt minimum
- Compliance with IPCC visual guidelines and UN branding where applicable
- Accessibility for color-blind readers using patterns or texture differentiation

## Recommended Chart Types
- [[Line Chart]] — Tracking emissions trajectories, temperature projections, and NDC pathways over time
- [[Area Chart]] — Showing cumulative emissions budgets and the narrowing gap to net-zero targets
- [[Waterfall Chart]] — Decomposing emission changes by sector or policy intervention impact
- [[Choropleth Map]] — Displaying country-level commitments, vulnerability indices, or per-capita emissions
- [[Stacked Bar Chart]] — Comparing emissions by gas type, sector breakdown, or national contributions

## Recommended Visual Styles
- [[Minimal]] — Clean lines and restrained decoration ensure the data speaks for itself, matching the sobriety expected in intergovernmental documents
- [[Corporate]] — Professional typography and structured layouts align with institutional publishing standards and convey authority

## Recommended Infographic Formats
- [[Statistical Infographic]] — Dense data presentation with multiple indicators, ideal for synthesis reports and technical summaries
- [[Geographic Infographic]] — Spatial data on climate impacts, adaptation needs, and regional commitments mapped to political boundaries

## Example Outputs
- IPCC Summary for Policymakers (SPM) headline statements with supporting figures
- UNFCCC Global Stocktake synthesis visualizations
- IEA Net Zero by 2050 pathway charts
- National NDC progress tracker dashboards
- Climate finance flow diagrams for COP presidency reports

### Energy & Climate Examples
- Global carbon budget remaining vs. 1.5C and 2C scenarios with uncertainty bands
- Sector-by-sector mitigation potential waterfall (energy, transport, industry, AFOLU)
- Regional grid decarbonization pathways comparing BAU, stated policies, and net-zero scenarios
- Loss and damage funding gap visualization across vulnerable nations
- Adaptation finance needs vs. actual flows by region and sector

## Tools & Platforms
- Adobe Illustrator for publication-grade vector graphics
- R (ggplot2) with IPCC-compliant theme packages for reproducible figures
- Datawrapper for quick, accessible web-embeddable charts with citation support
- QGIS or Mapbox for geospatial visualizations with custom projections

## Design Tips
> [!tip] Best Practices
> - Always include uncertainty ranges (shaded bands, error bars) to reflect scientific rigor
> - Use sequential single-hue palettes that degrade gracefully to grayscale for print
> - Place source citations directly below each figure, not in endnotes
> - Limit each figure to one core message; use panel figures for multi-dimensional comparisons
> - Test all visualizations at 50% zoom to ensure legibility in printed booklet formats

## Common Mistakes
> [!warning] Avoid These
> - Using rainbow color scales that obscure data patterns and fail for color-blind readers
> - Omitting base year or reference scenario, making trends uninterpretable
> - Overcrowding figures with too many countries or scenarios without clear hierarchy
> - Presenting projections without clearly distinguishing historical data from modeled futures
