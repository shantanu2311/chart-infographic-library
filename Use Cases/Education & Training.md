# Education & Training

## Overview
Education and training visualizations serve learners at various levels, from school students to professional workshop participants undergoing capacity building in climate and energy topics. These materials must build understanding progressively, moving from simple concepts to complex systems thinking. Interactivity, clarity, and cultural adaptability are more important than data density.

## Audience
- University and school students studying environmental science or sustainability
- Corporate employees undergoing carbon literacy or ESG training
- Government officials in capacity-building programs for climate reporting
- Workshop participants in GHG accounting or energy audit training
- Youth climate education program participants and educators

## Key Requirements
- Progressive complexity that scaffolds from foundational concepts to advanced analysis
- Interactive elements where possible (hover states, clickable drill-downs, sliders)
- Multilingual adaptability with text-light designs that minimize translation burden
- Consistent visual language across a learning module for cumulative comprehension
- Accommodation of diverse learning styles through varied chart types and annotation levels

## Recommended Chart Types
- [[Bar Chart]] — Introducing comparison concepts with clear, familiar visual forms that require minimal statistical literacy
- [[Line Chart]] — Teaching trend analysis and time-series interpretation with annotated inflection points and milestones
- [[Pie Chart]] — Simple proportion concepts for beginners, transitioning to more sophisticated alternatives in advanced modules
- [[Treemap Chart]] — Building systems thinking by showing nested hierarchies (global emissions by region, then sector, then source)

## Recommended Visual Styles
- [[Playful]] — Approachable, colorful aesthetics with rounded shapes and illustrations that reduce intimidation for non-technical learners
- [[Handwritten]] — Sketch-like, organic feel that creates a workshop atmosphere and signals "learning in progress" rather than final authority

## Recommended Infographic Formats
- [[Process Infographic]] — Step-by-step explanations of GHG accounting methodology, carbon cycle, or energy conversion chains
- [[Timeline Infographic]] — Historical context (industrial revolution to Paris Agreement) and future milestones for climate action

## Example Outputs
- UNEP climate change learning modules with layered interactive visualizations
- GHG Protocol Corporate Standard training slide decks with worked calculation examples
- Carbon literacy certification course materials with personal footprint breakdowns
- Youth climate education posters mapping cause-and-effect chains from emissions to impacts
- Energy audit workshop handouts with facility-level data collection templates and example charts

### Energy & Climate Examples
- Interactive carbon cycle diagram with clickable flows between atmosphere, ocean, biosphere, and lithosphere
- Step-by-step GHG calculation walkthrough (activity data x emission factor = CO2e) with visual annotation
- Energy unit conversion visualizer showing relationships between kWh, GJ, toe, and BTU
- Scope 1/2/3 boundary diagram with animated examples of each emission category
- Global warming potential comparison showing CO2 vs. CH4 vs. N2O impact over 20 and 100 year horizons

## Tools & Platforms
- H5P for creating interactive, embeddable learning objects with quiz integration
- Google Slides or PowerPoint for workshop presentation materials with speaker notes
- Genially for interactive infographics and gamified learning experiences
- Observable (D3.js notebooks) for explorable explanations with live code and adjustable parameters

## Design Tips
> [!tip] Best Practices
> - Start each module with the simplest possible visualization, adding complexity as concepts build
> - Use consistent iconography throughout a course so learners build visual vocabulary
> - Annotate charts heavily with callout boxes explaining what to look for and why it matters
> - Include "try it yourself" interactive elements where learners can change inputs and see results
> - Provide the same data in multiple chart types to teach visualization literacy itself

## Common Mistakes
> [!warning] Avoid These
> - Jumping to complex chart types (Sankey, radar) before establishing foundational chart literacy
> - Using jargon-heavy labels and legends without defining terms in the visualization itself
> - Creating static-only materials when the learning objective requires exploring data relationships
> - Assuming one visual style works across all age groups and cultural contexts
