# Social Media & Advocacy

## Overview
Social media and advocacy visualizations aim to distill complex climate and energy data into shareable, attention-grabbing graphics that drive public awareness and action. These must communicate a single clear message within seconds, work on small mobile screens, and be compelling enough to earn shares and engagement. The goal is not comprehensiveness but impact.

## Audience
- General public scrolling social media feeds
- Climate activists and advocacy organizations
- Journalists seeking embeddable graphics for articles
- Educators looking for shareable classroom materials
- Policymakers monitoring public discourse and sentiment

## Key Requirements
- Single clear message per graphic, comprehensible in under 5 seconds
- Mobile-first design optimized for vertical scrolling (1080x1350px or 1080x1080px)
- High contrast and large typography readable without zooming on phone screens
- Brand-safe with proper attribution and source citation in a compact footer
- Platform-specific formatting (Twitter cards, Instagram carousels, LinkedIn posts)

## Recommended Chart Types
- [[Bar Chart]] — Simple comparisons of emissions by country, sector, or fuel type with immediate visual hierarchy
- [[Donut Chart]] — Showing composition (energy mix, emission sources) with a bold center statistic for instant comprehension
- [[Waffle Chart]] — Making proportions tangible and visceral (e.g., "78 out of 100 scientists agree") for non-technical audiences
- [[Line Chart]] — Simplified trend lines (temperature anomaly, CO2 concentration) stripped to essential narrative with annotations

## Recommended Visual Styles
- [[Playful]] — Bold colors, rounded elements, and approachable aesthetics that feel native to social platforms
- [[Infographic]] — Strong visual hierarchy with icons, callout numbers, and minimal chart complexity for instant scanning

## Recommended Infographic Formats
- [[Statistical Infographic]] — Hero number plus supporting context, optimized for the "scroll-stopping" first impression
- [[List Infographic]] — Numbered lists ("5 ways to reduce your carbon footprint") that perform well as Instagram carousels

## Example Outputs
- Climate Council shareable graphics with single-stat hero numbers and source attribution
- Our World in Data embeddable charts reformatted for social sharing with simplified annotations
- Carbon footprint calculator result cards designed for Instagram Stories sharing
- COP countdown posts with daily key-stat graphics tracking negotiation milestones
- Side-by-side before/after comparisons of climate impacts (glacier retreat, sea level)

### Energy & Climate Examples
- "X degrees of warming" temperature strip graphics (warming stripes) as profile banners
- Country emissions rankings as horizontal bar races with per-capita normalization toggle
- Renewable energy milestone celebration posts (e.g., "Solar is now the cheapest electricity source")
- Interactive carbon footprint quiz results formatted as shareable story cards
- Weekly energy price tracker graphics comparing fossil fuel vs. renewable costs

## Tools & Platforms
- Canva for template-based social media graphics with brand kit consistency
- Flourish for animated and interactive charts embeddable across platforms
- Figma for custom-designed data visualizations with team collaboration
- Datawrapper for responsive, accessible charts that auto-adapt to embed contexts

## Design Tips
> [!tip] Best Practices
> - Lead with the most surprising or compelling number in oversized typography
> - Use no more than 3 colors per graphic to maintain clarity on small screens
> - Add a clear call-to-action or takeaway statement below the visualization
> - Test every graphic at actual phone size (hold your phone up) before publishing
> - Include a minimal source line (e.g., "Source: IEA 2025") to build credibility without clutter

## Common Mistakes
> [!warning] Avoid These
> - Packing too much data into a single graphic, defeating the scroll-stopping purpose
> - Using chart types that require explanation (Sankey, radar) for general audiences
> - Forgetting to include alt-text descriptions for accessibility on all platforms
> - Publishing without checking how the image crops on each platform's preview card
